Mozilla Manifesto

My Reflection

When I read the Mozilla Manifesto, what stood out to me most was the way it treats the internet as a public resource. That phrase really stuck with me because it reminds me that the web isn’t just a collection of apps or private spaces—it’s supposed to belong to everyone. I also appreciate how much they emphasize privacy and security; it feels like those ideas should be basic rights, but in practice they’re constantly challenged. The manifesto’s focus on community-driven innovation also resonated with me. I like the idea that the best parts of the internet come from people building things together, not just big companies setting the rules. Overall, reading it gave me a sense of responsibility to think about how I use the web and what kind of online world I want to help create.

Tim Berners-Lee’s “Long Live the Web”

My Reflection

Reading Berners-Lee’s essay really made me stop and think about how much of the web I take for granted. I like that he keeps coming back to the idea of universality—that anyone should be able to link to anything. It feels so obvious now, but without it the web would just be a patchwork of walled gardens. I also connected with his point on decentralization; I don’t want to have to ask for permission to publish a thought or a project. His reminder about open standards being royalty-free hit me too, because it’s easy to forget that behind the scenes, a ton of work went into making sure no single company “owns” the building blocks of the web. And honestly, his defense of net neutrality feels more relevant than ever—if internet providers start deciding what loads fast and what doesn’t, the whole spirit of a free and equal web breaks. Put together, his ideas push me to see the web less as just a convenience and more as a shared responsibility.

Both the manifesto and Berners-Lee’s essay reminded me that the web isn’t just a tool I use every day—it’s a shared space that only stays open and free if we keep fighting for it.
